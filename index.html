FLIP: From Design Thinking to Creative Confidence

Week 1: [Your Date]

Reading Focus: Chapter 1 ‚Äì "FLIP: From Design Thinking to Creative Confidence"

Key Concepts Learned: The Kelley brothers' "FLIP" concept ‚Äì a transformation from one state of mind to another. This chapter introduces the three-circle framework of design thinking: Feasibility (technical), Viability (business), and Desirability (human-centered). The Doug Dietz case study demonstrates how human-centered design can radically improve outcomes ‚Äì transforming an MRI scanner experience for pediatric patients.

Application to Learn The Money: I'm recognizing that financial literacy education has often emphasized feasibility and viability (teaching mechanics of budgeting) but has frequently overlooked desirability ‚Äì the human needs, motivations, and emotional barriers of learners. How might I "flip" my approach to prioritize understanding the fears and aspirations of teacher candidates and incarcerated women before designing curriculum?

Reflection: The growth mindset concept (Dweck) resonates deeply with my work. Many of my target learners have been told they're "not good with money" ‚Äì a fixed mindset belief. Creative confidence asks us to see this as a learnable skill. My project must help learners experience early wins to shift this belief.

Next Steps: This week, I'll conduct brief empathy interviews with 3-5 teacher candidates to understand their beliefs, fears, and perceptions around financial literacy teaching. I'll use this to reframe my curriculum design challenge.

SPARK: From Blank Page to Insight

Week 3: February 10, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 3 ‚Äì "SPARK: From Blank Page to Insight" in Creative Confidence by Kelley & Kelley.

Key Concepts Learned: This chapter emphasizes generating quantity over quality in early ideation, reframing challenges with "How might we..." questions, and using empathy interviews to uncover hidden user needs. The Embrace Infant Warmer case study showed how observing real users (not just data) led to a breakthrough design that addressed the actual context of rural mothers rather than just technical specifications.

Application to Learn The Money: Financial literacy education often focuses on delivering content ("teach compound interest") rather than understanding learner context. For my project, this means conducting empathy interviews with teacher candidates and incarcerated women to understand their actual financial pain points and decision-making contexts, not just their knowledge gaps. How might we design financial literacy modules that feel as intuitive and life-saving as the Embrace warmer felt to rural mothers?

Independent Connections: The chapter's emphasis on "choosing creativity" and "thinking like a traveler" aligns with Carol Dweck's growth mindset research and the d.school's design thinking methodology. I also connected it to Lusardi's financial literacy research, which shows that knowledge alone doesn't change behavior‚Äîcontextual understanding does.

Personal Reflection: The idea of "reframing challenges" hit home. I've been stuck thinking "How do I teach financial literacy to people with money trauma?" Now I'm reframing it as "How might we create financial confidence through tiny, safe experiments?" This shift opens up prototyping ideas like one-week money challenges or peer feedback circles.

Next Steps: This week I'll conduct 3 empathy interviews with teacher candidates about their financial teaching anxieties. I'll use "How might we..." questions to reframe their challenges and prototype one low-stakes financial activity for the Learn The Money curriculum.

Journal Entry Template (15/5 Format)

Use this structure for your weekly entries to ensure consistency and depth:

15/5 Format Reminder: Each entry should be approximately 15 minutes to write and take colleagues ~5 minutes to read. Focus on clarity, substance, and actionable insights.

Entry Structure:

1. Reading Focus (1-2 sentences)
Identify the specific chapter and primary concepts from the week's reading assignment.

2. Key Concepts Learned (2-3 key ideas)
Synthesize 2-3 major themes from the reading. Include definitions and cite the authors. This demonstrates understanding of core concepts.

3. Application to My Project (How does this apply to Learn The Money?)
Make explicit connections between reading concepts and your financial literacy curriculum development work. Explore "how might we..." questions.

4. Independent Findings or Connections (Optional)
Include any LinkedIn Learning courses, articles, or resources you discovered that relate to the week's topic. Cite these appropriately in APA format.

5. Personal Reflection (Your thinking)
What did this reading make you think about? How does it challenge or affirm your current approach?

6. Next Steps or Questions (Action-oriented)
What will you do this week to practice or apply these concepts? What questions do you still have?
