Financial Literacy & Creative Confidence

Integrating Design Thinking with the Learn The Money Initiative

🎯 Project Abstract

Learn The Money: A human-centered financial literacy curriculum designed through design thinking methodology for teacher candidates and underserved populations. By applying Creative Confidence principles, this project transforms financial education from rote knowledge transmission to confidence-building, context-aware learning experiences that address real learner needs—money trauma, decision paralysis, and systemic barriers.

The goal: Equip educators with tools to foster financial self-efficacy in their students and communities through a 10-week design journey documented in this journal.

Welcome to My Design Journal

This design journal documents my learning journey through Creative Confidence: Unleashing the Creative Potential Within Us All by David and Tom Kelley, and its applications to instructional design for financial literacy education.

Course Focus: I am exploring how design thinking methodologies and creative confidence principles can enhance curriculum development for the Learn The Money financial literacy initiative, with particular emphasis on serving teacher candidates and underserved populations.

15/5 Format: Each entry is designed to take approximately 15 minutes to write and 5 minutes to read, following best practices for reflective practice in instructional design.

ENTRY 1

FLIP: From Design Thinking to Creative Confidence

Week 1: January 26, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 1 – "FLIP: From Design Thinking to Creative Confidence".

Key Concepts Learned: The Kelley brothers describe “FLIP” as the shift from seeing creativity as rare to seeing it as a learnable capacity. The chapter introduces the three-circle design thinking framework—Feasibility, Viability, Desirability—and uses the Doug Dietz MRI case to show how human‑centered design can dramatically change experiences for vulnerable users.

Application to Learn The Money: Traditional financial literacy often stays in feasibility (math, content) and viability (products) while ignoring desirability—the fears, hopes, and lived realities of learners. For Learn The Money, I need to flip my own practice so I start with empathy for teacher candidates and incarcerated women, then design content around their real contexts rather than abstract standards.

Reflection: The growth‑mindset lens helps me see “I’m bad with money” as a belief, not a fixed trait. If I expect creative confidence from my learners, I also have to model it by sharing in‑progress work and inviting feedback instead of waiting until everything is polished.

Next Steps: I will conduct short empathy interviews with 3–5 teacher candidates to surface their money stories, anxieties, and prior experiences. These insights will guide my first round of prototype activities.

📚 Citation: Kelley & Kelley (2013), Ch. 1

ENTRY 2

DARE: From Fear to Courage

Week 2: February 2, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 2 – "DARE: From Fear to Courage" in Creative Confidence.

Key Concepts Learned: The authors reframe fear of failure as a normal part of creative work and highlight Bandura’s guided‑mastery research, showing how confidence grows through a series of small, successful steps. Creative people do not fail less; they run more experiments and interpret each attempt as learning rather than personal judgment.

Application to Learn The Money: Many learners in my programs carry money trauma and shame, which makes them fearful of public mistakes. I want to design low‑stakes practice—tiny financial experiments like a one‑week spending log or a $5 automated transfer—that allow learners to experience quick, concrete wins and start rewriting their internal stories about being “bad with money.”

Independent Connections: This chapter connects directly to Bandura’s self‑efficacy work and Dweck’s growth mindset research. In a financial‑literacy context, that means building structures that help learners see evidence of their own competence, not just hear explanations from an expert.

Personal Reflection: I recognized my own perfectionism as a fear pattern—I delay curriculum prototypes because I want them to be publication‑ready. DARE nudges me to treat each version of Learn The Money as a prototype and to make my “messy middle” visible rather than hiding it.

Next Steps: I will design one “safe‑to‑fail” role‑play where teacher candidates practice a difficult money conversation, then debrief what felt empowering or uncomfortable. I’ll ask each participant to name one small money‑related risk they are now willing to take in their own life or teaching.

📚 Citation: Kelley & Kelley (2013), Ch. 2; Bandura (1997); Dweck (2006)
⏱️ Read time: ~5 min
ENTRY 3

SPARK: From Blank Page to Insight

Week 3: February 10, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 3 – "SPARK: From Blank Page to Insight".

Key Concepts Learned: The chapter emphasizes generating lots of ideas before evaluating them, reframing challenges with “How might we…?” questions, and using field observations and empathy interviews to uncover needs that data alone can’t reveal. The Embrace Infant Warmer case demonstrates how reframing from “low‑cost incubator” to “baby‑warming solution for rural mothers” opened up an entirely new product direction.

Application to Learn The Money: Instead of asking “How do I teach all the required money topics?”, I’m reframing to “How might we create financial confidence through small, meaningful experiments?” This pushes me to observe real contexts—teacher‑candidate classrooms, correctional‑education settings—and design activities that fit those realities.

Independent Connections: SPARK echoes design‑thinking practices from the d.school and aligns with Lusardi’s research showing that context and behavior matter as much as content knowledge. It also reminds me of lean‑startup idea flow: the more ideas you surface, the more likely you are to find a high‑leverage one.

Personal Reflection: I saw how often I open a blank document and wait for the “perfect” curriculum idea instead of using prompts, constraints, or rough sketches to get started. Adopting a traveler’s mindset—noticing friction points in everyday money experiences—gives me more raw material to design from.

Next Steps: This week I will conduct three empathy interviews with teacher candidates, focusing on moments when they felt stuck or successful with money. I’ll turn their stories into at least five “How might we…?” questions and use those as seeds for new Learn The Money activities.

📚 Citation: Kelley & Kelley (2013), Ch. 3; Lusardi (2010); d.school resources
⏱️ Read time: ~5 min
ENTRY 4

LEAP: From Planning to Action

Week 4: February 16, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 4 – "LEAP: From Planning to Action" in Creative Confidence.

Key Concepts Learned: This chapter describes the “knowing–doing gap,” where teams talk about innovation but hesitate to test ideas in the real world. The Pulse news‑app story illustrates how tight deadlines and constant user feedback pushed the founders to prototype quickly in a café rather than perfecting a long business plan. The authors argue that small, fast experiments are more valuable than detailed plans that never get implemented.

Application to Learn The Money: I realized I’ve been treating parts of Learn The Money like a planning problem—collecting articles, frameworks, and citations without enough live testing. LEAP reframes my constraints (busy teacher candidates, limited class time, complex money trauma) as design fuel. Instead of waiting until a whole module is “ready,” I can run tiny pilots: one 15‑minute activity, a quick confidence check‑in, or a single prototype handout tested with a small group.

Independent Connections: LEAP connects to lean‑startup ideas about minimum viable products and iterative learning with customers. It also mirrors the gap in financial literacy where people already “know” they should budget or save but struggle to act. That insight reinforces Lusardi’s findings that knowledge alone is insufficient; I need to design for realistic, doable money actions that fit learners’ daily lives.

Personal Reflection: I saw myself in the people who wait for the perfect moment or perfect plan before starting. My tendency to want polished, branded curricula before sharing is really a fear of visible failure. LEAP pushes me to measure progress by experiments run, not documents drafted.

Next Steps: This week I will prototype one “Money Micro‑Experiment” activity that teacher candidates can run with peers or family—such as a three‑day spending‑awareness challenge. I’ll collect quick feedback using two prompts (“What helped you feel more confident?” and “What would you change?”) and use that data to refine the activity for incarcerated learners and community workshops.

📚 Citation: Kelley & Kelley (2013), Ch. 4; Lusardi (2010)
⏱️ Read time: ~5 min
ENTRY 5

SEEK: From Duty to Passion

Week 5: March 2, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 5 – "SEEK: From Duty to Passion" in Creative Confidence by Kelley & Kelley, alongside Patrick Parrish's (2006) "Design as Storytelling."

Key Concepts Learned: This chapter argues that the most sustainably creative people are not those who grind through obligation, but those who have found genuine intersection between their skills, curiosity, and the needs of others. The Kelleys introduce the idea of "the zone of genius"—work that energizes rather than depletes you. Parrish's design‑storytelling framework adds a parallel insight: powerful instructional experiences are not built from content outlines alone, but from vivid, empathetic narratives written from the learner's perspective. Both frameworks push the designer to ask not just "What should I teach?" but "What does this experience feel like from the inside for the person living it?"

Application to Learn The Money: I realized that my most energized design moments for Learn The Money happen when I'm connecting financial literacy to human stories—María's rent‑vs‑debt dilemma, a teacher candidate's childhood overdraft memory, an incarcerated woman building a reentry budget. Those are not just examples; they are the emotional engine of the curriculum. SEEK pushed me to lean into that instinct rather than defaulting to a content‑coverage checklist. Using Parrish's design‑story approach, I drafted a first‑person learner narrative (published in the Design Story section of this site) that places me inside the experience of a teacher candidate encountering Learn The Money for the first time—sitting in the back row, nervous, relieved, and finally shifting from "I'm bad with money" to "I can host better money conversations."

Independent Connections: The SEEK chapter resonates with Ikigai, the Japanese concept of finding purpose at the intersection of what you love, what you are good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. For financial literacy education, this framework is directly applicable: learners who see money management as aligned with their values and goals (rather than a duty imposed from outside) are far more likely to sustain new behaviors. This connects to Lusardi's research showing that motivation and self‑relevance are stronger predictors of financial behavior change than knowledge alone. Parrish's storytelling lens reinforces the same point—narrative creates self‑relevance in ways that bullet‑pointed content cannot.

Personal Reflection: Writing the design story was unexpectedly difficult. I kept slipping out of the learner's voice and back into the instructor's voice—analyzing the curriculum instead of inhabiting it. That tension revealed something important: I default to the "expert" stance because it feels safer than admitting vulnerability. But Learn The Money only works if I design from empathy first and expertise second. SEEK and Parrish together reminded me that passion in instructional design shows up as the willingness to keep rewriting until the learner's experience feels true, not just correct.

Next Steps: I will revise the Money Micro‑Experiment activity using the design‑story insights: specifically, I'll add a brief "money feelings" warm‑up at the start of each module that invites learners to name an emotion before engaging with any numbers. I will also draft a second design story written from the perspective of an incarcerated woman encountering the reentry budgeting module, to stress‑test whether the curriculum's tone and assumptions hold across very different life contexts.

📚 Citation: Kelley & Kelley (2013), Ch. 5; Parrish (2006); Lusardi (2010)

ENTRY 6

TEAM: Creatively Confident Groups

Week 6: March 3, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 6: "TEAM: Creatively Confident Groups" in Creative Confidence, with a focus on how teams create psychological safety, diverse idea flow, and shared ownership of prototypes.

Key Concepts Learned: The Kelley brothers argue that creative work is almost always a team sport. Strong teams deliberately build psychological safety so people feel safe sharing half‑baked ideas, they balance diverse perspectives instead of privileging a single "expert," and they create rituals like rapid brainstorming or story‑sharing that keep ideas moving. Leaders act more like gardeners than heroes: their job is to create conditions where others can flourish, not to have all the answers.

Application to Learn The Money: Learn The Money cannot depend on one charismatic facilitator; it needs teams of teacher candidates, community educators, and even learners who co‑create experiences. That means building norms and structures that make money conversations feel safe in a group. For example, I can embed specific team rituals into the curriculum: a "no‑shame" rule that is revisited every session, rounds where everyone shares one small experiment they tried, and rotating roles storyteller, listener, note‑taker so quieter participants still have defined ways to contribute.

Independent Connections: This chapter connects to Amy Edmondson’s work on psychological safety and to research on professional learning communities in teacher education. In financial literacy, teams matter because learners are trying to change habits inside social systems, families, peer groups, workplaces. If the team around them does not support new behaviors, confidence erodes quickly. Designing for teams means designing for how groups talk about money, not just how individuals calculate it.

Personal Reflection: I often treat curriculum design as a solo project, then later ask for "feedback" when most major decisions are already locked in. That pattern limits real collaboration. TEAM pushes me to involve others much earlier—inviting teacher candidates, correctional educators, and formerly incarcerated women to react to rough ideas, not just polished lesson plans. It also challenged me to notice who is not in the room when I’m making design choices and how that shapes blind spots.

Next Steps: Over the next two weeks, I will pilot a small "design council" for Learn The Money: a group of 3–5 people, including at least one teacher candidate and one community educator, who meet briefly to react to new Money Micro‑Experiments. I will test two practices from the chapter: starting with a quick round of appreciations to lower defensiveness, and capturing all suggestions visually before evaluating any of them. My goal is to see whether these structures increase the variety of ideas and help participants feel more ownership of the curriculum.

📚 Citation: Kelley & Kelley (2013), Ch. 6; Edmondson (1999)
⏱️ Read time: ~5 min
ENTRY 7

MOVE: Creative Confidence to Go

Week 7: March 10, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 7: "MOVE: Creative Confidence to Go" in Creative Confidence, which focuses on sustaining creative momentum beyond a single project or class and integrating design habits into everyday life.

Key Concepts Learned: MOVE emphasizes that creative confidence is a practice, not a one‑time achievement. The authors describe small, repeatable habits, such as capturing ideas in a notebook, regularly reframing problems, and running quick experiments that keep people in motion instead of sliding back into inertia. Storytelling and reflection are critical: when people articulate what they tried and what they learned, they are more likely to keep going.

Application to Learn The Money: For learners in Learn The Money, the real test comes after the workshop ends. If they leave with only a binder of materials, nothing will change. MOVE pushes me to design "creative confidence to go" elements into the curriculum: simple Money Micro‑Experiments they can continue on their own, reflection prompts they can reuse with new financial decisions, and check‑in routines they can adapt with their students. For teacher candidates, this might look like a monthly "money conversation" circle in their future classrooms, built around quick stories and small commitments rather than long lectures.

Independent Connections: This chapter resonates with habit‑formation research (Clear, 2018) and with self‑regulated learning frameworks in education. Both suggest that small, consistent actions beat rare, intense efforts. In financial literacy, this aligns with ideas like automatic savings, debt snowballs, or weekly money dates structures that make follow‑through easier than avoidance. Embedding these patterns into Learn The Money could help learners see themselves as people who regularly experiment with money choices instead of waiting for a crisis.

Personal Reflection: My own work on Learn The Money has come in bursts around deadlines, followed by quiet periods. MOVE challenged me to define what a sustainable creative practice looks like: short, scheduled blocks each week to document one insight, update one activity, or run one conversation. It also reminded me that telling the story of my progress as I’m doing in this public journal is itself a way to keep moving.

Next Steps: I will design a one‑page "Creative Confidence To Go" handout for Learn The Money participants. It will include three tiny Money Micro‑Experiments, three reflection questions, and a simple plan for a 20‑minute monthly money check‑in. I’ll pilot this with teacher candidates first and ask them, one month later, which elements they actually used. Their feedback will shape how I integrate ongoing habits into future iterations of the curriculum, especially in correctional‑education and community settings.

📚 Citation: Kelley & Kelley (2013), Ch. 7; Clear (2018)

Extended Artifact Explanation

Artifact overview. This web‑based design journal documents the iterative development of Learn The Money, a human‑centered financial literacy curriculum informed by design thinking and the framework in Creative Confidence. Across ten weeks, the site traces how ideas move from theory to prototype to implementation in ways that are transparent, research‑grounded, and teachable to others.

Potential audiences. The primary audience is teacher candidates and practicing educators who want to integrate financial literacy into their classrooms but feel underprepared or anxious about teaching money topics. The journal offers them a concrete example of how an educator wrestles with ambiguity, fear, and institutional constraints while still moving a project forward. A second audience is higher‑education faculty and instructional designers in teacher preparation, adult education, or community‑based programs; for them, the artifact serves as a live case study in applying design‑thinking practices—empathy interviews, reframing, and rapid prototyping—to a complex socio‑emotional domain like money. A third audience includes community partners and stakeholders connected to Learn The Money (correctional education programs, nonprofits, funders) who need a clear explanation of what makes this curriculum distinct from traditional budgeting workshops. Finally, the journal also contributes to a broader professional portfolio for immigration and career review panels by demonstrating a coherent research‑to‑practice trajectory.

Problem or opportunity. The artifact responds to two overlapping gaps. On the learner side, there is a persistent “knowing–doing gap” in financial literacy: adults can often explain concepts like budgeting or compound interest but still feel stuck, ashamed, or helpless about their own money decisions. Traditional programs emphasize content coverage and quizzes while underestimating money trauma, shame, and learned helplessness—especially among underserved populations such as incarcerated women or students from chronically under‑resourced communities. On the educator side, many preservice and in‑service teachers care deeply about financial capability but lack curricular resources, pedagogical models, and personal confidence. They may regard money as too personal, too political, or beyond their role. Together, these gaps represent both a problem (missed opportunities to build financial agency) and an opportunity to design learning experiences that center confidence and context, not just information.

How the artifact addresses the need. First, the design journal models a design‑thinking stance applied to financial literacy. Each entry uses a consistent 15/5 format (reading focus, key concepts, application, independent connections, reflection, next steps), making visible how abstract ideas—guided mastery, growth mindset, rapid experimentation—are translated into specific changes in activities, facilitation, and assessment. Instead of presenting polished “final” lessons, the artifact normalizes iterative experimentation and safe‑to‑fail tests that educators can adapt in their own settings.

Second, the journal keeps learners at the center by continually foregrounding specific populations: teacher candidates, incarcerated women, college students, and community members carrying money trauma. Entries explicitly connect course concepts and research (e.g., Bandura on self‑efficacy, Dweck on mindset, Lusardi on financial capability) to concrete Learn The Money scenarios. This shows how human‑centered design uncovers barriers that purely cognitive curricula miss—for example, fear of public disclosure around debt or a belief that being “bad with money” is a fixed identity rather than a changeable skill set.

Third, the artifact creates a transparent narrative of growth over time. By archiving early assumptions, evolving questions, and course corrections, it pushes back against perfectionism and highlights that meaningful curriculum emerges from many small leaps rather than a single breakthrough. Constraints such as limited class time, institutional expectations, and learners’ life circumstances are treated as design prompts rather than excuses to maintain the status quo. For educators and stakeholders who visit the site, this narrative communicates that Learn The Money is not just another static workshop but an evolving, research‑informed effort to build financial confidence and agency among people who have historically been underserved by both education and financial systems.

Design Story: First Day in Learn The Money

Perspective: Teacher candidate experiencing the Learn The Money curriculum for the first time.

I’m sitting in the back row, close enough to the door that I can leave if I need to. I tell myself I’m just here for the extra credit, but my stomach knows better. Any time someone says “financial literacy,” my chest tightens. I’ve made too many mistakes with money to pretend this is neutral content.

The room feels different from what I expected. There’s no big slide that says “Budgeting 101.” Instead, the screen shows the words Money Stories and a simple prompt: When did money first feel powerful in your life? The facilitator another grad student, not some distant expert explains that today is about curiosity, not judgment. I’m not sure I believe them yet, but I notice that my shoulders drop a little.

We start with a quick anonymous poll on our phones. “On a scale of 1–5, how confident do you feel talking about money with your students or future students?” I tap 2 and feel a tiny wave of shame, imagining everyone else picking 4 or 5. The live results appear: most people are clustered around 2 and 3. I’m surprised, then relieved. I’m not the only one who feels underprepared.

Next, we listen to a short story about a learner named “María,” a first‑generation college student trying to decide between paying down credit card debt and helping family with rent. As the story unfolds, I feel myself leaning forward. I recognize the tension between being responsible and being loyal, between numbers on a spreadsheet and people you love. The facilitator doesn’t tell us what María “should” do. Instead, they ask, “What feelings do you think she’s carrying into a classroom conversation about money?” I jot down: fear of judgment, pride, fatigue, and a quiet hope that someone might finally understand.

Then comes the part I dreaded: sharing. But the structure makes it feel safer. We work in pairs with a simple protocol from the Learn The Money site. One person answers three prompts, “My earliest money memory…,” “One belief I was taught about money…,” and “A time money felt out of control…” while the other just listens and reflects back emotions, not advice. We’re told to stay in the present tense: “I feel,” “I notice,” “I’m thinking,” as if the memory is happening right now.

When it’s my turn, I almost default to a joke, but my partner waits quietly. I hear myself say, “I’m eight years old, standing with my mom at the customer‑service desk while she argues about an overdraft fee.” As I describe the scene, I can feel the fluorescent lights, the crumpled receipt, my mom’s voice tightening. I realize that a part of me still believes banks are like principals’ offices, places you only go when you’re in trouble.

My partner doesn’t rush to fix it. She says, “I hear how small and responsible you feel at the same time.” That sentence lands deeper than any financial tip I’ve heard in years. I notice that this activity isn’t about budgeting yet; it’s about making space for the emotions we drag into budgeting.

Later, we shift into designing. The facilitator introduces a “Money Micro‑Experiment” card from the Learn The Money curriculum: a three‑day spending‑awareness challenge we can adapt for our own contexts. In small groups, we rewrite the instructions so they would make sense for different learners—ninth‑graders, community‑college students, or incarcerated women. We have to decide: What tone would feel respectful? What examples would feel real, not performative? We keep coming back to our own money stories as we argue about wording.

As we work, I notice a shift. I’m not just a student being taught about money; I’m a designer thinking about how money feels to other people. When someone suggests adding a “no shame” rule to the challenge, I feel a quiet click of recognition. If I’m this nervous in a graduate class, how must my future students feel?

We end with a brief reflection in our journals, guided by prompts from the website: “Right now, I understand that money conversations in my teaching…” and “A small change I’m willing to test next week is…” I write, “I understand that money conversations are really identity conversations. Next week, I’ll prototype a five‑minute ‘money feelings’ warm‑up before any numbers.” For the first time, that idea feels doable instead of overwhelming.

Walking out of the session, I still don’t feel like a financial expert. But I notice that my inner story has shifted from “I’m bad with money” to “I am learning how to host better money conversations.” I can picture my future students more clearly, not as blank slates I must fill with content, but as people carrying their own María‑like stories into the room.

On the way home, I re‑read this design story on the Learn The Money site. Seeing the session through my own first‑person narrative helps me notice where the curriculum is working—reducing shame, inviting reflection—and where it still needs to be softer, more honest, and more practical. The story becomes a design tool: if a future activity doesn’t respect the feelings I just lived on the page, it probably needs to change.
