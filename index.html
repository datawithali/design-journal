🎯 Project Abstract

Learn The Money: A human-centered financial literacy curriculum designed through design thinking methodology for teacher candidates and underserved populations. By applying Creative Confidence principles, this project transforms financial education from rote knowledge transmission to confidence-building, context-aware learning experiences that address real learner needs—money trauma, decision paralysis, and systemic barriers.

The goal: Equip educators with tools to foster financial self-efficacy in their students and communities through a 10-week design journey documented in this journal.

Tentative Research Plan: Learn The Money

Overview. This tentative research plan positions Learn The Money as a foundation for future design-based research, pilot testing, and possible dissertation or grant development. Rather than treating the current artifact as a finished product, this plan frames it as the most developed prototype to date—one that can support systematic data collection, iterative redesign, and deeper study of how financial-literacy learning environments build confidence, reflection, and agency among teacher candidates and community-based learners.

Research focus. The overarching purpose of the study is to examine how a human-centered financial literacy curriculum can support not only conceptual understanding of money topics, but also financial self-efficacy, willingness to engage in money conversations, and epistemic agency. In alignment with the project’s conjecture map, the study asks how specific design features—tools and materials, task structures, participant structures, and discursive practices—shape learner experience and outcomes over time.

Guiding research questions. Three initial questions guide this plan: (1) How does participation in Learn The Money influence learners’ financial self-efficacy and confidence in discussing money-related topics? (2) Which features of the curriculum design appear to support or constrain learner reflection, emotional safety, and real-world transfer? (3) How do iterative revisions, informed by learner data and conjecture mapping, improve the effectiveness of the intervention across cycles?

Theoretical framework. This study is grounded in design-based research and guided by conjecture mapping, drawing especially on the idea that learning environments embody hypotheses about how learning happens. The project also builds on Wilson’s Four Pillars of instructional design by attending not only to cognition, but also to context, moral-value dimensions, and aesthetic experience. Together, these frameworks support a view of Learn The Money as both an instructional intervention and a theory-building design experiment.

Proposed methodology. The study will begin with a small pilot cycle involving teacher candidates or adult learners who participate in selected Learn The Money activities, such as Money Micro-Experiments, reflective prompts, or structured money-story discussions. A mixed-methods approach will be used: quantitative measures will provide evidence of change in confidence or self-efficacy, while qualitative data will help explain how participants experience the design. Across multiple DBR cycles, findings will be used to revise activities, facilitation moves, and assessment strategies.

Participants and data sources. The initial participant pool will likely include teacher candidates, graduate students, or community-based adult learners because these groups are the most feasible for early pilot work. Possible data sources include pre/post surveys, short reflective journal responses, observational notes, learner artifacts, facilitator memos, and feedback on prototype activities. Later phases could extend to community workshops or additional underserved populations as the curriculum and research protocol mature.

Qualtrics integration. TAMU-CC’s Qualtrics account offers a practical infrastructure for this work. It can be used to create pre/post surveys, confidence check-ins, reflection prompts, and learner feedback forms using multiple question types, branching logic, display logic, and downloadable data exports. In the context of Learn The Money, Qualtrics could support a simple research workflow: a pre-assessment before participation, brief embedded feedback after prototype activities, and a post-assessment to document changes in confidence, perceived relevance, or willingness to apply financial practices in daily life.

Potential measures. One especially useful option is a short financial self-efficacy instrument paired with custom reflection items related to money shame, classroom confidence, and action planning. These data could be triangulated with learner work samples and facilitator observations to identify both measurable trends and richer explanatory patterns. In this way, the research would not rely on surveys alone, but would treat survey data as one layer of evidence inside a broader iterative design process.

Anticipated contribution. This project has the potential to contribute to instructional design and educational technology by showing how financial literacy can be designed as a confidence-building, context-aware, and emotionally responsive learning experience rather than only a content-delivery problem. By integrating Qualtrics-based pre/post assessment with DBR iterative cycles and conjecture mapping, the project models an accessible, low-cost research infrastructure for graduate student researchers studying community-facing educational interventions.

FLIP: From Design Thinking to Creative Confidence

Week 1: January 26, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 1 – "FLIP: From Design Thinking to Creative Confidence".

Key Concepts Learned: The Kelley brothers describe “FLIP” as the shift from seeing creativity as rare to seeing it as a learnable capacity. The chapter introduces the three-circle design thinking framework—Feasibility, Viability, Desirability—and uses the Doug Dietz MRI case to show how human‑centered design can dramatically change experiences for vulnerable users.

Application to Learn The Money: Traditional financial literacy often stays in feasibility (math, content) and viability (products) while ignoring desirability—the fears, hopes, and lived realities of learners. For Learn The Money, I need to flip my own practice so I start with empathy for teacher candidates and incarcerated women, then design content around their real contexts rather than abstract standards.

Reflection: The growth‑mindset lens helps me see “I’m bad with money” as a belief, not a fixed trait. If I expect creative confidence from my learners, I also have to model it by sharing in‑progress work and inviting feedback instead of waiting until everything is polished.

Next Steps: I will conduct short empathy interviews with 3–5 teacher candidates to surface their money stories, anxieties, and prior experiences. These insights will guide my first round of prototype activities.

DARE: From Fear to Courage

Week 2: February 2, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 2 – "DARE: From Fear to Courage" in Creative Confidence.

Key Concepts Learned: The authors reframe fear of failure as a normal part of creative work and highlight Bandura’s guided‑mastery research, showing how confidence grows through a series of small, successful steps. Creative people do not fail less; they run more experiments and interpret each attempt as learning rather than personal judgment.

Application to Learn The Money: Many learners in my programs carry money trauma and shame, which makes them fearful of public mistakes. I want to design low‑stakes practice—tiny financial experiments like a one‑week spending log or a $5 automated transfer—that allow learners to experience quick, concrete wins and start rewriting their internal stories about being “bad with money.”

Independent Connections: This chapter connects directly to Bandura’s self‑efficacy work and Dweck’s growth mindset research. In a financial‑literacy context, that means building structures that help learners see evidence of their own competence, not just hear explanations from an expert.

Personal Reflection: I recognized my own perfectionism as a fear pattern—I delay curriculum prototypes because I want them to be publication‑ready. DARE nudges me to treat each version of Learn The Money as a prototype and to make my “messy middle” visible rather than hiding it.

Next Steps: I will design one “safe‑to‑fail” role‑play where teacher candidates practice a difficult money conversation, then debrief what felt empowering or uncomfortable. I’ll ask each participant to name one small money‑related risk they are now willing to take in their own life or teaching.

📚 Citation: Kelley & Kelley (2013), Ch. 2; Bandura (1997); Dweck (2006)

Adopting a traveler’s mindset—noticing friction points in everyday money experiences—gives me more raw material to design from.

Next Steps: This week I will conduct three empathy interviews with teacher candidates, focusing on moments when they felt stuck or successful with money. I’ll turn their stories into at least five “How might we…?” questions and use those as seeds for new Learn The Money activities.

LEAP: From Planning to Action

Week 4: February 16, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 4 – "LEAP: From Planning to Action" in Creative Confidence.

Key Concepts Learned: This chapter describes the “knowing–doing gap,” where teams talk about innovation but hesitate to test ideas in the real world. The Pulse news‑app story illustrates how tight deadlines and constant user feedback pushed the founders to prototype quickly in a café rather than perfecting a long business plan. The authors argue that small, fast experiments are more valuable than detailed plans that never get implemented.

Application to Learn The Money: I realized I’ve been treating parts of Learn The Money like a planning problem—collecting articles, frameworks, and citations without enough live testing. LEAP reframes my constraints (busy teacher candidates, limited class time, complex money trauma) as design fuel. Instead of waiting until a whole module is “ready,” I can run tiny pilots: one 15‑minute activity, a quick confidence check‑in, or a single prototype handout tested with a small group.

Independent Connections: LEAP connects to lean‑startup ideas about minimum viable products and iterative learning with customers. It also mirrors the gap in financial literacy where people already “know” they should budget or save but struggle to act. That insight reinforces Lusardi’s findings that knowledge alone is insufficient; I need to design for realistic, doable money actions that fit learners’ daily lives.

Personal Reflection: I saw myself in the people who wait for the perfect moment or perfect plan before starting. My tendency to want polished, branded curricula before sharing is really a fear of visible failure. LEAP pushes me to measure progress by experiments run, not documents drafted.

Next Steps: This week I will prototype one “Money Micro‑Experiment” activity that teacher candidates can run with peers or family—such as a three‑day spending‑awareness challenge. I’ll collect quick feedback using two prompts (“What helped you feel more confident?” and “What would you change?”) and use that data to refine the activity for incarcerated learners and community workshops.

TEAM: Creatively Confident Groups

Week 6: March 3, 2026

Reading Focus: Chapter 6 – "TEAM: Creatively Confident Groups" in Creative Confidence, with a focus on how teams create psychological safety, diverse idea flow, and shared ownership of prototypes.

Key Concepts Learned: The Kelley brothers argue that creative work is almost always a team sport. Strong teams deliberately build psychological safety so people feel safe sharing half‑baked ideas, they balance diverse perspectives instead of privileging a single "expert," and they create rituals—like rapid brainstorming or story‑sharing—that keep ideas moving. Leaders act more like gardeners than heroes: their job is to create conditions where others can flourish, not to have all the answers.

Application to Learn The Money: Learn The Money cannot depend on one charismatic facilitator; it needs teams of teacher candidates, community educators, and even learners who co‑create experiences. That means building norms and structures that make money conversations feel safe in a group. For example, I can embed specific team rituals into the curriculum: a "no‑shame" rule that is revisited every session, rounds where everyone shares one small experiment they tried, and rotating roles—storyteller, listener, note‑taker—so quieter participants still have defined ways to contribute.

Independent Connections: This chapter connects to Amy Edmondson’s work on psychological safety and to research on professional learning communities in teacher education. In financial literacy, teams matter because learners are trying to change habits inside social systems—families, peer groups, workplaces. If the team around them does not support new behaviors, confidence erodes quickly.

Personal Reflection: I often treat curriculum design as a solo project, then later ask for "feedback" when most major decisions are already locked in. That pattern limits real collaboration. TEAM pushes me to involve others much earlier—inviting teacher candidates, correctional educators, and formerly incarcerated women to react to rough ideas, not just polished lesson plans.

Next Steps: Over the next two weeks I will pilot a small "design council" for Learn The Money: a group of 3–5 people—including at least one teacher candidate and one community educator—who meet briefly to react to new Money Micro‑Experiments. I will test practices like starting with appreciations and capturing all suggestions visually before we evaluate any of them.

📚 Citation: Kelley & Kelley (2013), Ch. 6; Edmondson (1999)

Integration: Design Thinking in Financial Literacy

Key Concepts Learned: Across the book, the Kelley brothers frame creative confidence as a practice grounded in empathy, rapid experimentation, and collaborative teams. The design‑based research literature—especially Sandoval’s conjecture mapping and Wilson’s Four Pillars of instructional design—adds a second layer: every learning environment already embodies a theory of how learning happens. Making those conjectures explicit through maps and logic models turns design work into something testable and improvable instead of a collection of isolated activities.

Application to Learn The Money: For Learn The Money, this means treating the entire curriculum and this public journal as an evolving design conjecture. Each Money Micro‑Experiment, each narrative activity, and each facilitation move is not just a “lesson idea,” but part of a larger hypothesis about how financial confidence develops for teacher candidates and underserved adults.

Prototype & Iterate: Testing Creative Approaches

Week 9: March 24, 2026

Reading Focus: This week’s work focused on prototype testing and iteration across the Creative Confidence framework, especially the shift from generating promising ideas to testing them in real educational contexts. I also connected this phase to my design conjecture map and logic model so that each prototype could be treated as a test of a larger learning hypothesis.

Key Concepts Learned: Prototyping is not just about making an activity visible; it is about making assumptions visible. A prototype gives learners something concrete to respond to, which is often more useful than asking them abstract questions about what they might like. Iteration then becomes the disciplined practice of revising the design in response to what learners actually do, say, and produce, rather than defending the designer’s original intention.

Application to Learn The Money: In Learn The Money, this means I cannot judge a financial-literacy activity only by whether the instructions are clear or the content is accurate. I need to test whether the activity actually lowers shame, invites reflection, and helps learners make more confident decisions about money. For example, a Money Micro‑Experiment may look strong on paper, but if learners experience it as too personal, too abstract, or too time-consuming, then the design needs revision. The prototype is successful only if it works in the emotional and social reality of the learners, not just in my planning document.

Independent Connections: This week helped me see a stronger link between classroom prototyping and design-based research. In the conjecture map, the prototype is part of the embodiment of design, while learner conversations, revised worksheets, and reflection responses become evidence of mediating processes. That means when I test a money-story prompt, peer-discussion protocol, or spending-awareness challenge, I am not just improving an activity; I am gathering evidence about whether my broader conjecture is working—namely, that authentic, reflective, low-stakes financial tasks can build confidence and epistemic agency over time.

Personal Reflection: I noticed that I still have a tendency to become attached to activities I spent a long time designing. When feedback suggests that something is confusing or emotionally heavy, my first instinct is to explain the activity better rather than rethink it. This entry reminded me that iteration requires humility. If learners misread or resist a prototype, that is not a failure of the learner; it is valuable information about the design. In that sense, critique is not a threat to my work but one of the most useful parts of the process.

Next Steps: Over the next week, I will test at least one Learn The Money prototype with a small group and document three kinds of evidence: what learners say, what they produce, and what they avoid. I will then revise the activity using those findings and note which part of the conjecture map the revision is addressing—tools and materials, task structure, participant structure, or discursive practice. My goal is to make Entry 10 not just a reflection on what I learned, but a clearer account of how iterative testing changed the curriculum itself.

Extended Artifact Explanation

Artifact overview. This web‑based design journal documents the iterative development of Learn The Money, a human‑centered financial literacy curriculum informed by design thinking and the framework in Creative Confidence. Across ten weeks, the site traces how ideas move from theory to prototype to implementation in ways that are transparent, research‑grounded, and teachable to others.

Potential audiences. The primary audience is teacher candidates and practicing educators who want to integrate financial literacy into their classrooms but feel underprepared or anxious about teaching money topics. The journal offers them a concrete example of how an educator wrestles with ambiguity, fear, and institutional constraints while still moving a project forward. A second audience is higher‑education faculty and instructional designers in teacher preparation, adult education, or community‑based programs; for them, the artifact serves as a live case study in applying design‑thinking practices to a complex socio‑emotional domain like money. A third audience includes community partners and stakeholders connected to Learn The Money (correctional education programs, nonprofits, funders) who need a clear explanation of what makes this curriculum distinct from traditional budgeting workshops. Finally, the journal also contributes to a broader professional portfolio by demonstrating a coherent research‑to‑practice trajectory.

Problem or opportunity. The artifact responds to two overlapping gaps. On the learner side, there is a persistent “knowing–doing gap” in financial literacy: adults can often explain concepts like budgeting or compound interest but still feel stuck, ashamed, or helpless about their own money decisions. Traditional programs emphasize content coverage and quizzes while underestimating money trauma, shame, and learned helplessness—especially among underserved populations such as incarcerated women or students from chronically under‑resourced communities. On the educator side, many preservice and in‑service teachers care deeply about financial capability but lack curricular resources, pedagogical models, and personal confidence. Together, these gaps represent both a problem and an opportunity to design learning experiences that center confidence and context, not just information.

How the artifact addresses the need. First, the design journal models a design‑thinking stance applied to financial literacy. Each entry uses a consistent 15/5 format (reading focus, key concepts, application, independent connections, reflection, next steps), making visible how abstract ideas—guided mastery, growth mindset, rapid experimentation—are translated into specific changes in activities, facilitation, and assessment. Instead of presenting polished “final” lessons, the artifact normalizes iterative experimentation and safe‑to‑fail tests that educators can adapt in their own settings.

Second, the journal keeps learners at the center by continually foregrounding specific populations: teacher candidates, incarcerated women, college students, and community members carrying money trauma. Entries explicitly connect course concepts and research to concrete Learn The Money scenarios. This shows how human‑centered design uncovers barriers that purely cognitive curricula miss—for example, fear of public disclosure around debt or a belief that being “bad with money” is a fixed identity rather than a changeable skill set.

Third, the artifact creates a transparent narrative of growth over time. By archiving early assumptions, evolving questions, and course corrections, it pushes back against perfectionism and highlights that meaningful curriculum emerges from many small leaps rather than a single breakthrough. Constraints such as limited class time, institutional expectations, and learners’ life circumstances are treated as design prompts rather than excuses to maintain the status quo. For visitors, this narrative communicates that Learn The Money is not just another static workshop but an evolving, research‑informed effort to build financial confidence and agency among people who have historically been underserved by both education and financial systems.

High‑Level Design Conjectures

Why a conjecture map? Following Sandoval’s conjecture‑mapping framework, this project treats the Learn The Money curriculum as an explicit set of hypotheses about how financial‑literacy learning happens for teacher candidates and underserved adults. Rather than treating sessions as disconnected activities, the conjecture map and logic model specify how design features are expected to work together to produce confidence, deeper understanding, and epistemic agency around money.

High‑level conjecture. Learners develop deeper conceptual understanding, collaborative inquiry skills, and epistemic agency when the learning environment is designed around authentic, reflective practice that integrates cognitive scaffolding (Wilson’s Pillar 1), real‑world contextual connections (Pillar 2), moral‑value awareness (Pillar 3), and aesthetic experience (Pillar 4), enacted through structured collaborative inquiry with iterative design and argumentation tasks.

Embodiment in Learn The Money. In this project, the conjecture is embodied through four types of design elements:

  • Tools & materials: collaborative digital tools, Money Micro‑Experiment cards, money‑story prompts, and authentic financial scenarios that make abstract concepts concrete and emotionally resonant.
  • Task structures: iterative design tasks, epistemic reflection prompts, structured argumentation cycles, and peer‑review protocols that push learners to revise ideas in response to evidence.
  • Participant structures: small collaborative groups, teacher‑as‑facilitator, and distributed expertise roles that create communities of practice and shared ownership.
  • Discursive practices: norms for evidence‑based reasoning, justification, consensus‑building dialogue, and narrative sharing that foreground issues of access, power, and voice.

Key design conjectures. Several working design conjectures guide the current iteration of Learn The Money:

  • Conjecture A: If learners engage in iterative Money Micro‑Experiments using authentic tools within small groups that share epistemic responsibility, then they will produce stronger evidence‑based justifications and engage in more generative arguments about financial choices.
  • Conjecture B: If discursive norms for consensus‑building, “no‑shame” storytelling, and demands for justification are consistently enacted, then learners will internalize healthier epistemic standards for talking about money and apply them spontaneously beyond the course.
  • Conjecture C: If tasks are structured to connect explicitly to real‑world contexts and roles (future teachers, returning citizens, caregivers), then learners will perceive financial literacy as relevant, increasing motivation and quality of knowledge application.
  • Conjecture D: If aesthetic dimensions—emotionally resonant narratives, clear visual design, and thoughtfully paced experiences—are deliberately designed, then learners will experience deeper, more memorable engagement that supports conceptual change and sustained confidence.

Mediating processes and outcomes. These design conjectures focus attention on specific mediating processes: collaborative epistemic negotiation, metacognitive reflection on inquiry, real‑world connection making, and sustained uptake of discursive norms. Observable artifacts—argumentation products, revised Money Micro‑Experiments, reflection journals, and peer feedback—serve as proxies for whether the conjectures are playing out. The theoretical conjecture is that, if these mediating processes occur, they will lead to outcomes such as deeper conceptual understanding of money, transferable epistemic agency, critical awareness of social and value dimensions, and sustained motivation to engage with financial decisions.

Logic‑model alignment. A complementary logic model translates this design theory into program‑level planning and evaluation: inputs (facilitators, tools, time) support activities (collaborative inquiry labs, argumentation workshops, reflection sessions) that yield outputs (design iterations, argumentation products, attendance and journal counts). These outputs are expected to drive short‑term outcomes (knowledge, skills, attitudes), intermediate outcomes (transfer of inquiry skills to new money contexts), and long‑term impacts (ethical, aesthetically aware instructional design and lasting financial agency for learners).

Using the conjectures going forward. Future iterations of Learn The Money will treat this conjecture map and logic model as living documents. As new data from classes and community settings come in, design conjectures will be revised, added, or discarded. The aim is not to freeze the curriculum, but to create a transparent, theoretically grounded trail showing how design decisions change in response to evidence and how those changes affect learners’ financial confidence and epistemic agency.

Design Story: First Day in Learn The Money

Perspective: Teacher candidate experiencing the Learn The Money curriculum for the first time.

I’m sitting in the back row, close enough to the door that I can leave if I need to. I tell myself I’m just here for the extra credit, but my stomach knows better. Any time someone says “financial literacy,” my chest tightens. I’ve made too many mistakes with money to pretend this is neutral content.

The room feels different from what I expect. There’s no big slide that says “Budgeting 101.” Instead, the screen shows the words Money Stories and a simple prompt: When did money first feel powerful in your life? The facilitator—another grad student, not some distant expert—explains that today is about curiosity, not judgment. I’m not sure I believe them yet, but I notice that my shoulders drop a little.

We start with a quick anonymous poll on our phones: “On a scale of 1–5, how confident do you feel talking about money with your students or future students?” I tap 2 and feel a tiny wave of shame, imagining everyone else picking 4 or 5. The live results appear: most people are clustered around 2 and 3. I’m surprised, then relieved. I’m not the only one who feels underprepared.

Next, we listen to a short story about a learner named María, a first‑generation college student trying to decide between paying down credit card debt and helping family with rent. As the story unfolds, I feel myself leaning forward. I recognize the tension between being responsible and being loyal, between numbers on a spreadsheet and people you love. The facilitator doesn’t tell us what María “should” do. Instead, they ask, “What feelings do you think she’s carrying into a classroom conversation about money?” I jot down: fear of judgment, pride, fatigue, and a quiet hope that someone might finally understand.

Then comes the part I dreaded: sharing. But the structure makes it feel safer. We work in pairs with a simple protocol from the Learn The Money site. One person answers three prompts—“My earliest money memory…,” “One belief I was taught about money…,” and “A time money felt out of control…”—while the other just listens and reflects back emotions, not advice. We’re told to stay in the present tense: “I feel,” “I notice,” “I’m thinking,” as if the memory is happening right now.

When it’s my turn, I almost default to a joke, but my partner waits quietly. I hear myself say, “I’m eight years old, standing with my mom at the customer‑service desk while she argues about an overdraft fee.” As I describe the scene, I can feel the fluorescent lights, the crumpled receipt, my mom’s voice tightening. I realize that a part of me still believes banks are like principals’ offices—places you only go when you’re in trouble.

My partner doesn’t rush to fix it. She says, “I hear how small and responsible you feel at the same time.” That sentence lands deeper than any financial tip I’ve heard in years. I notice that this activity isn’t about budgeting yet; it’s about making space for the emotions we drag into budgeting.

Later, we shift into designing. The facilitator introduces a Money Micro‑Experiment card from the Learn The Money curriculum: a three‑day spending‑awareness challenge we can adapt for our own contexts. In small groups, we rewrite the instructions so they would make sense for different learners—ninth‑graders, community‑college students, or incarcerated women. We have to decide: What tone would feel respectful? What examples would feel real, not performative? We keep coming back to our own money stories as we argue about wording.

As we work, I notice a shift. I’m not just a student being taught about money; I’m a designer thinking about how money feels to other people. When someone suggests adding a “no shame” rule to the challenge, I feel a quiet click of recognition. If I’m this nervous in a graduate class, how must my future students feel?

We end with a brief reflection. On an index card, we answer two questions: “What is one belief about money you want to examine more closely?” and “What is one tiny experiment you’re willing to try this week?” I write, “Belief: If I make a money mistake, it proves I’m irresponsible. Experiment: Spend 20 minutes looking at my bank app without judging myself—just noticing.” It feels almost laughably small, but also strangely radical.

Walking out, I notice that my chest feels lighter. Nothing about my bank account has changed in ninety minutes, but something about the story I tell myself has. For the first time, I can imagine bringing money into my future classroom not as a unit to survive, but as a conversation to design—carefully, collaboratively, and without shame.
